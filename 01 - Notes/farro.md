## Description
Farro is a grain similar to Bulghur wheat or couscous.
### Best Farro Alternatives for a Tomato & Olive Seafood Dish:

#### 1. Orzo (Risoni)

- **Why it works:** Pasta-like texture but similar bite to farro; holds up beautifully with tomato sauces and seafood.
    
- **Bonus:** Quick-cooking and ideal for loose, layered dishes.
    

#### 2. Pearl Barley

- **Why it works:** Chewy texture like farro, soaks up broth or sauce well. Gives that rustic feel.
    
- **Note:** Slightly longer cook time, but it’s worth it.
    

#### 3. Israeli Couscous (Ptitim)

- **Why it works:** Toasted pasta pearls with a springy bite; great for layering and absorbs flavors like a dream.
    
- **Vibe:** Light but toothsome—great with seafood and olives.
    

#### 4. Short-Grain Brown Rice

- **Why it works:** Has that stickier, denser bite similar to farro, and adds a nutty depth.
    
- **Note:** Longer cook time, but solid flavor carrier.
    

#### 5. Fregola Sarda

- **Why it works:** Toasted Sardinian pasta balls with nutty notes. Excellent texture and a regional match for seafood and olives.
    
- **Vibe:** Fancy-casual Mediterranean.
    

#### 6. Bulgur (Coarse)

- **Why it works:** Quick-cooking and nutty, not too soft. Might be a little looser than farro but plays well in layered dishes.
    
- **Note:** Avoid fine bulgur—it'll turn mushy.
    

#### 7. Quinoa (if going gluten-free)

- **Why it works:** Soaks up sauce, brings a lighter touch. Not chewy like farro, but works well flavor-wise.
    
- **Bonus:** Fast-cooking, GF-friendly.

###  How to Use in an Open Lasagne:

For that open-style seafood lasagne:

- **Layer** with the grain or pasta as your "base" or interspersed between layers of seafood, olives, tomato sauce, and herbs.
    
- Think of the grain as the **textural anchor**—you want it to stand up to the juicy seafood and robust sauce.
