# Lifestyle Bucket

## What is it?
The Lifestyle Bucket is the buffer between cash needed to spend day-to-day supporting retirement and the investment growth engine; the Longevity Bucket. It also helps bridge the funding gap initially between retiring and the state pension, but its primary purpose is provide a stable source of funds to top up Liquidity if markets perform badly, allowing the growth engine to recover. Lifestyle bucket aims to grow moderate returns, sacrificing growth for stability over the medium horizon in case needed. 

If 'more' liquidity is needed to fund an unexpected expense. Example home repairs, a large purchase or larger than usual holiday. This is the bucket I draw from. It's also designed to be a shock absorber for market down turns, providing options.

👉 This bucket is there to:

- Be **steadier than equities**
- Still **earn something above cash**
- Be **easy to access when needed**
- Avoid forcing you to sell growth assets at the wrong time

The Lifestyle bucket isn’t about maximising return—it’s about reducing the number of decisions you have to make when conditions are uncertain.

Bonds and multi-asset funds are simply tools that sit in that space between cash and equities. Not exciting, not particularly fast-growing, but steady enough that you can rely on them without having to think too hard about timing.

They don’t need to outperform. They just need to behave.

## 🥈 Lifestyle (3–7 years)

- Stability + optionality
- Some return, low volatility
- You _might_ spend this

👉 “This protects my lifestyle if markets misbehave”

## Once per year:

1. Check balance
2. If you need money:
    - Sell a portion (usually bonds or multi-asset)
3. Refill Liquidity bucket

### What funds make up Lifestyle bucket
Principally:
- **Bonds** (stability anchor) - Example types you’ll see on Halifax
	- iShares Core Global Aggregate Bond UCITS ETF
	- Vanguard UK Government Bond Index Fund
	- iShares £ Corporate Bond UCITS ETF
- **Multi-asset Funds** - One fund that already contains shares, bonds and sometimes property. Examples: Halifax calls these "mixed investment funds". Slightly higher costs, but paying for simplicity.
	- Vanguard LifeStrategy 40% Equity Fund
	- HSBC Global Strategy Balanced Portfolio
	- 7IM AAP Balanced Fund

(Halifax explicitly categorises these as “mixed investment funds”
- **NS&I Premium Bonds** - Part of the buffer layer. Can be accessed in downturns. Can refill liquidity pot. Think of them as a shock-absorber with bonus yield (monthly prize). Ideally draw down these last.
### Your Lifestyle bucket might look like:

- Bonds / short-duration funds
- Multi-asset funds
- **Premium Bonds (~£40–50k)**
#### 🧭 How to combine them (practical setup)

For your Lifestyle bucket (~£90k–£120k):

**Example structure**

- **40% Bonds** → stability
- **40% Multi-asset fund** → balanced growth
- **20% Premium Bonds** → tax-free buffer

---

👉 Why this mix works:

- Bonds = anchor
- Multi-asset = gentle growth
- Premium Bonds = safe reserve
## 🧩 Putting it together (realistic Lifestyle bucket)

### Example £130k Lifestyle allocation
This materially represents 8 years of buffer if I assume £15k is needed to top up Liquidity bucket each year on top of the defined benefit annual pension annuity.

£130k / £15k = 8.6 Years. This is more than required for the bridging strategy. 

- £40k → Bond ETF (core stability)
- £40k → Multi-asset fund (balanced growth)
- £50k → Premium Bonds (deep reserve)
### Drawdown order within Lifestyle:

1. 🟡 Bonds / funds (first)
2. 🟡 Multi-asset
3. 🟢 **Premium Bonds (last)**

**Premium Bonds** sit in an interesting place. They feel like something you want to hold onto—not because they grow aggressively, but because they don’t demand anything of you.

They don’t fall when markets fall. They don’t generate taxable income. And they don’t force a decision.

That makes them less of an engine, and more of a stabiliser. Something that sits quietly in the background, only becoming relevant when everything else is under pressure.

And in that sense, they belong firmly in the Lifestyle bucket—not because you plan to spend them, but because they’re there for when you might have to.

👉 They act as your **deep reserve within the buffer**
# 📊 When would you actually use them?

Only if:

- Markets are down **AND**
- Other defensive assets are depleted

👉 That’s rare — but exactly why they’re valuable

# ⚠️ One important nuance

## Don’t overfill Lifestyle

Target:
- ~4–6 years of spending gap

👉 For you:
- ~£80k–£120k total
